Almost every FYP proposal includes a Gantt chart, and almost every one of them is wrong by week three. Not because students are bad planners, but because the chart gets built to satisfy a proposal template requirement rather than to actually predict how the semester will go. Then reality hits — a component takes two weeks to arrive from Shopee instead of three days, your supervisor is on leave the exact week you needed sign-off, or the "two-day" coding task turns into ten — and the chart becomes irrelevant paperwork instead of a working plan.

## Why the Standard Template Chart Fails

Most students build their Gantt chart by taking the standard FYP phases — literature review, methodology, hardware procurement, development, testing, documentation — and dividing them evenly across the semester. It looks tidy, but it assumes every phase takes a predictable, fixed amount of time and that nothing ever blocks anything else. Neither is true for a hardware-based FYP, where procurement delays, component failures, and supervisor availability all create dependencies a straight-line chart doesn't capture.

## Build the Timeline Backwards From Your Real Deadlines

Start from your actual fixed dates — proposal defence, progress presentation, final report submission, viva — not from week one. These are the dates you cannot move, so everything else has to fit around them, with buffer, not right up against them.

**1. Mark your fixed institutional deadlines first.** Pull these directly from your faculty's FYP handbook or coordinator's announcement, not from memory — dates shift slightly between cohorts and it's a bad place to be wrong.

**2. Work backwards to set your "must be working" date.** Your final demo needs a functioning prototype, not a finished one, roughly two to three weeks before the actual submission — that buffer is for fixing whatever breaks during rehearsal, and it should be non-negotiable in your own planning even if it isn't written into the official schedule.

**3. Add explicit procurement buffer, not zero.** If you're ordering sensors or modules from Shopee or Lazada, budget five to seven working days minimum, and double that if any component needs to come from an overseas seller. Order everything you'll need in one batch as early as your proposal is approved, not component-by-component as you reach each stage — reordering mid-project because you forgot a part is one of the most common causes of lost weeks.

**4. Schedule your supervisor meetings into the chart, not around it.** If your supervisor requires sign-off before you can proceed to the next phase (common for proposal and methodology chapters), that approval time is a real task with its own duration — usually three to seven days depending on how responsive your supervisor is — and it belongs on the chart as its own bar, not folded invisibly into "writing."

**5. Separate "build" from "debug" as two different bars.** Students consistently underestimate debugging time because it isn't a task with a clear starting technique — you don't know what's broken until you're already in it. As a rule of thumb, budget debugging time roughly equal to build time for anything involving sensor calibration, wireless communication, or new hardware you haven't used before.

## What a More Honest Chart Looks Like

Instead of five even blocks, a realistic hardware FYP timeline usually has this shape: a longer-than-expected first phase (literature review and proposal approval, since this depends on supervisor turnaround you don't control), a tight middle phase with parallel tracks (hardware build and report-writing happening simultaneously rather than sequentially), and a wide safety margin before your hard deadline rather than a chart that ends exactly on submission day.

If you're using Microsoft Project, Excel, or a free tool like a Google Sheets Gantt template to build yours, the tool matters far less than whether the dates are based on your actual calendar constraints rather than an evenly divided semester.

## What to Do When You Fall Behind Anyway

Even a well-built chart will slip — that's normal, not a failure. The difference between students who recover and those who don't is usually whether they re-plan immediately when they notice the slip, rather than continuing to follow a chart they already know is wrong.
